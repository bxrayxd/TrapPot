\section*{Abstract}
\end{center}

\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{Abstract}

\begin{spacing}{1.5}
IoT devices are increasingly used in Saudi Arabia's smart-city rollout. Exposed remote-access services, weak credentials, and limited patching create the same type of risk Mirai exploited in 2016, when it reached roughly 600,000 infected devices \cite{203628}. TrapPot is a system we built to study that problem directly inside a controlled lab network.

The system has four layers. Cowrie listens on ports 2222 (SSH) and 2223 (Telnet), emulates a vulnerable embedded Linux device, and logs every login attempt, command, and file download. Zeek shares Cowrie's network namespace and converts the resulting traffic into structured \texttt{conn.log} and \texttt{ssh.log} records. An AI detector reads twelve features from \texttt{conn.log}, loads a trained Random Forest model (\texttt{trappot\_model.pkl}), and classifies each connection as brute-force, scanning, malware download, command execution, or normal traffic. Logstash, Elasticsearch, and Kibana handle indexing and present the data in the TrapPot Overview dashboard.

We trained the classifier on the IoT-23 dataset. After fixing a class-imbalance problem with SMOTE oversampling (the first three runs came in around 72\%), the model reached 99.59\% accuracy on the 20\% hold-out set. We also deployed and tested the full stack locally via Docker Compose, confirming that Cowrie, Zeek, the AI detector, Elasticsearch, Logstash, and Kibana worked as a connected pipeline. TrapPot does not just score well on a dataset; it runs.

{\bf Keywords:} TrapPot, Internet of Things (IoT), Honeypot, Random Forest (RF), Cybersecurity, Artificial Intelligence (AI).
\end{spacing}
